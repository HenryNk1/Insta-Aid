Asthma

Diagnosis

Asthma is usually diagnosed through a combination of a medical history, physical exam, and tests. If you have symptoms like shortness of breath, wheezing, or persistent coughing, a healthcare provider will assess your condition to confirm if it's asthma.


Tests Here are common tests used to diagnose asthma:


  • Spirometry: This test measures how much air you can exhale after a deep breath and how fast you can breathe out. It's used to check for airway narrowing or obstruction.

  • Peak flow test: A peak flow meter is a simple device that measures how hard you can breathe out. Lower-than-normal readings suggest your lungs may not be working well, indicating asthma.

  • Methacholine challenge: This test involves inhaling a substance called methacholine, which can cause airway constriction in people with asthma. If your lung function drops after inhaling methacholine, it’s a sign of asthma.

  • Allergy tests: Skin or blood tests are used to identify allergens that may be triggering asthma symptoms.

  • Exhaled nitric oxide test: This test measures the amount of nitric oxide, a marker of inflammation, in your breath. Higher levels may indicate asthma.

  • Chest X-ray or CT scan: These are typically used to rule out other conditions, like infections or lung diseases, that might be causing your symptoms.

Early diagnosis helps with effective management and treatment, reducing the risk of severe asthma attacks.


Treatment

Asthma treatment focuses on managing symptoms, preventing attacks, and maintaining normal lung function. Asthma can be controlled with proper medication and by avoiding triggers that cause symptoms.


Medications: There are two main types of asthma medications: quick-relief medications for sudden symptoms and long-term control medications for daily management.


  • Quick-Relief (Rescue) Inhalers: These inhalers, like albuterol, are used during an asthma attack to quickly open the airways and relieve symptoms such as wheezing, coughing, or shortness of breath.

  • Corticosteroids (Inhaled): These are the most common long-term asthma medications. They help reduce inflammation in the airways, making it easier to breathe over time.

  • Leukotriene Modifiers: These oral medications reduce airway inflammation and can prevent asthma symptoms for up to 24 hours.

  • Long-Acting Beta Agonists (LABAs): These inhalers are used daily to control chronic asthma symptoms and help prevent future attacks.

  • Combination Inhalers: These inhalers contain both a long-acting beta-agonist and a corticosteroid for better long-term control.

  • Allergy Medications: If asthma is triggered by allergies, antihistamines or other allergy medications may help manage symptoms.

Lifestyle and Environmental Control


  • Avoiding Triggers: Knowing and avoiding asthma triggers (such as dust, pollen, pet dander, and smoke) is key to preventing asthma attacks.

  • Regular Monitoring: Using a peak flow meter to track lung function can help in recognizing early signs of an asthma flare-up before symptoms worsen.

  • Healthy Lifestyle: Staying active, managing stress, and maintaining a healthy diet can all help improve overall lung health and minimize asthma symptoms.


How to Assist Someone During an Asthma Attack Before Medical Help Arrives

If someone is having an asthma attack, it’s important to stay calm and help them manage their symptoms quickly. Here’s what you can do:


  1. Sit the person upright: Help them sit upright in a comfortable position. Encourage them to remain calm, as panic can make breathing more difficult.

  2. Use their inhaler:

    • If they have a rescue inhaler (usually a blue inhaler), assist them in using it. Shake the inhaler and have them take one puff, inhaling slowly and deeply.

    • Have them wait 30-60 seconds between puffs. They can take up to 4 puffs if needed.

  3. Loosen tight clothing: If their clothing is restrictive, help loosen it to make breathing easier.

  4. Keep them calm: Reassure them to breathe slowly and deeply. This helps prevent hyperventilation, which can worsen symptoms.

  5. Monitor symptoms:

    • If symptoms don't improve within 5-10 minutes of using the inhaler, or if they worsen, seek emergency help immediately.

    • If they don't have their inhaler, call for emergency help right away.

  6. Call for medical help:

    • Dial your local emergency number if the inhaler is not working or if the person’s condition is getting worse.

    • Look for signs of severe distress, such as difficulty speaking, bluish lips, or drowsiness. These indicate a medical emergency.
