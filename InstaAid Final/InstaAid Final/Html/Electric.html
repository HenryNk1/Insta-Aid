Electric Shock

Diagnosis

Electric shock can vary in severity, from minor burns to life-threatening internal damage. Diagnosis in a medical setting involves assessing the visible injuries and determining whether the electric shock has caused deeper internal damage, such as heart issues or nerve damage.


Test Here are common tests used to diagnose the extent of damage from an electric shock:


  • Physical Exam: The healthcare provider will check for burns, entry and exit wounds, and any signs of trauma caused by the shock.

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG): This test records the heart’s electrical activity. Since electric shock can affect heart rhythms, an ECG is used to check for irregularities or damage.

  • Blood Tests: These tests check for signs of muscle damage, kidney injury, or the release of certain chemicals, such as potassium, that may occur after an electric shock.

  • Imaging Tests (X-ray, CT, or MRI): Depending on the severity of the shock, these tests may be done to check for fractures, internal injuries, or damage to organs.

  • Neurological Evaluation: If the shock has affected the brain or nervous system, neurological tests may be used to assess cognitive and motor functions.

Treatment

Treatment for electric shock depends on the severity of the injury. In minor cases, burns or superficial wounds may be treated, while severe electric shocks may require intensive care.


Medications: Medications used to treat electric shock injuries may include:


  • Pain Relievers: These help manage pain from burns or other injuries.

  • Antibiotics: If the electric shock caused burns or open wounds, antibiotics may be prescribed to prevent infection.

  • Cardiac Medications: If the shock affected the heart, medications like beta-blockers or antiarrhythmics may be needed to stabilize heart rhythm.

  • IV Fluids: Intravenous fluids help maintain blood pressure and support kidney function, especially if muscle damage is significant.

Surgical Procedures: If severe burns or tissue damage occurred, surgery may be necessary:


  • Debridement: This procedure removes damaged tissue to promote healing and prevent infection.

  • Skin Grafts: If burns are extensive, skin grafts may be used to repair damaged areas.

  • Cardiac Surgery: In rare cases, surgery may be required if the heart sustained damage that medication cannot treat.

How to Assist Someone During an Electric Shock Before Medical Help Arrives

If someone has been electrocuted, here’s what you can do to help them before medical help arrives:


  1. Turn off the power source: Do not touch the person until you are sure the source of electricity is turned off. Use a non-conductive object (like a wooden broom handle) to move them away from the source if you can’t turn it off.

  2. Check for a response: Once the power source is off, check if the person is conscious and breathing. If they are unresponsive, call for emergency help immediately.

  3. Call emergency services: Dial your local emergency number and inform them of the situation. Be ready to describe the person's condition.

  4. Do not move them: If the person has fallen or been thrown by the shock, there could be potential spine or head injuries. Only move them if they are in immediate danger.

  5. Start CPR if necessary:

    • If the person is not breathing or does not have a pulse, begin CPR. Push hard and fast in the center of their chest at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute. Continue until medical help arrives or they begin to breathe on their own.

    • If you are trained in rescue breathing, alternate 30 chest compressions with 2 breaths.

  6. Check for burns or injuries: Once the immediate danger has passed, assess the person for burns at the entry and exit points of the electrical current. If there are burns, cover them with a sterile cloth or bandage to prevent infection.

  7. Keep them warm: If the person is conscious, cover them with a blanket to keep them warm and comfortable while waiting for medical help.

  8. Reassure and monitor: Stay with the person, keep them calm, and monitor their breathing and pulse until medical professionals arrive.

Remember: Electric shock can cause internal damage even if external injuries seem minor. Always seek medical help after an electric shock, even if the person appears to recover quickly.
